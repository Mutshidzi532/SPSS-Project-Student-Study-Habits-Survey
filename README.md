# SPSS-Project-Student-Study-Habits-Survey
# Student Study Habits and Academic Performance Survey

## Introduction
This project analyzes survey data collected from 20 university students on academic and personal study factors, including age, gender, study hours, internet usage, concentration, and academic performance.  
The objective is to explore patterns that may influence student outcomes and provide insights into study behaviors.

## Methodology
- Data was collected from students in a classroom setting, selecting a minimum of 20 participants with no repetitions.  
- The dataset includes both demographic information and self-reported study habits.  
- Analysis was conducted using **SPSS**, including descriptive statistics, graphical visualizations, and correlation analysis.

## Descriptive Analysis
- **Age:** 18–26 years (mean = 22.22), representing a young adult university population.  
- **Gender:** Nearly balanced (mean = 1.45), indicating a slight majority of one gender depending on coding.  
- **Study Hours:** Average 38.78 hours/week (SD = 16.88), showing large variation.  
- **Academic Marks:** Average 64.41% (range 33–89%), also highly variable.  
- **Time Management & Concentration:** Rated 0–2; average scores 0.85 and 1.05, indicating moderate self-assessment.

## Graphical Analysis
- **Bar Chart (Hours of Study vs. Academic Marks):** Students with higher average marks generally reported more study hours, suggesting a positive but variable relationship.  
- **Pie Chart (Overall Concentration):** Concentration levels are evenly split among “Poor”, “Fair”, and “Good”, reflecting diversity in attention skills.

## Correlation Analysis
- Pearson correlation between Questionnaire ID and Age: r = 0.064, p = 0.802 (N ≈ 18).  
- Interpretation: Extremely weak linear association; high p-value indicates insufficient evidence to reject the null hypothesis.  
- Notes:  
  - Small sample size reduces statistical power.  
  - Non-significant results do not imply no effect, only that no effect was reliably detected.  
  - Visual inspection or non-parametric alternatives (e.g., Spearman’s rho) may provide additional insights.  

## Conclusion
- The study shows high variability in study hours and academic performance among students.  
- Positive trends exist between study time and academic marks, but individual differences are significant.  
- Future studies with larger sample sizes are recommended to better estimate population relationships.

## Tools Used
- **SPSS**: Data cleaning, descriptive statistics, correlation analysis, and visualization.
